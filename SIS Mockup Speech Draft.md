# SIS Tournament Revamp - Mockup Speech Draft

Working tone: direct, commercial, and practical. The goal is not to sell a new esports idea. The goal is to show that reliability is a revenue lever, and that the six-week NBA trial is the safest way to prove it.

## Opening

Today I want to walk through a focused proposal for SIS Competitive Gaming.

The point is simple: every cancelled match is revenue lost. The current operating model gives us volume, but it does not give gamers enough reason to protect reliability shift after shift. This proposal changes that. It connects the league format, the reward model, and the progression system into one operating loop.

I am not asking for a full estate-wide change on day one. I am asking for a six-week NBA operating strategy trial, using a controlled cell, with clear gates for expansion.

## Slide 1 - Cover

I would open by framing this as an operating model, not a cosmetic tournament refresh.

The working title is H2H League Format. The important part is the logic behind it: matches need stakes, gamers need progression, and the business needs fewer cancellations. If those three things line up, the product becomes more reliable and more valuable.

## Slide 2 - The Business

SIS Competitive Gaming is sportsbook-grade live inventory. It is always-on, it gives operators familiar markets, and it fills the gaps between traditional sport.

That means reliability is not a back-office detail. If a match does not run, the sportsbook does not get the betting moment, the viewer loses rhythm, and SIS loses the revenue attached to that event.

So the NBA trial matters because it tests the operating layer underneath the public product promise.

## Slide 3 - The Problem

The problem is cancellations.

Using the current working assumption, each cancellation represents about 1,000 pounds of revenue lost. If we are losing around 15 matches per shift, that becomes 30 per day, 210 per week, and roughly 10.9 million pounds across a full year.

That number is why this needs to be treated as a revenue problem, not just a staffing issue.

## Slide 4 - One Percentage Point

This slide is the clearest commercial lever.

One percentage point of reliability improvement means about 2,200 cancellations avoided across the year. At the 1,000 pounds revenue-loss baseline, that is about 2.2 million pounds protected.

At three points, it is 6.6 million. At five points, it is 11 million.

The key message is that we do not need a perfect model to create value. Even a small reliability gain is worth serious money.

## Slide 5 - Why The Current Model Fails

The current model creates four problems.

First, burnout. If the shift structure is too repetitive or too heavy, mistakes and absences increase.

Second, no real progression. If gamers do not feel they are moving anywhere, they have less reason to stay engaged.

Third, flat reward. If the reward system mostly pays attendance, it does not directly price the behavior that protects the feed.

Fourth, schedule fragility. When one gamer drops out, the issue can become a wider cancellation problem.

The format is producing the behavior we are trying to reduce.

## Slide 6 - Three Pillars

The proposal has three pillars: format, reward, and progression.

Format gives every shift a clear competition structure. Reward pays for wins and reliability, not just hours. Progression gives gamers a visible career path from Academy through Champion.

The important point is that these are not separate initiatives. They work together. The format creates stakes, the reward system prices the right behavior, and progression makes that behavior visible over time.

## Slide 7 - Tournament Structure

This is where the H2H League Format becomes tangible.

The NBA version is the first operating test. It uses tiers, movement, and promotion/relegation logic so that every shift has consequence.

The future version can travel to EAFC and eAMF, but only after NBA proves the model. That keeps the ask disciplined: prove one title first, then expand.

## Slide 8 - Shift Cycle

This slide explains the operating rhythm.

Every shift starts from the previous standings. Gamers play the scheduled matches. Results update the table. The top performers move up, the bottom performers move down, and the next shift starts with a new set of stakes.

That is the game loop. It gives gamers a reason to care about today because today affects tomorrow.

## Slide 9 - Progression

Progression turns reliability into a career signal.

Academy is the entry point. Rookie, Regular, Specialist, Elite, and Champion create visible steps. The score is not just who won one match. It is SPS, reliability, settlement accuracy, and sustained form.

The core idea is that a reliable gamer should not just be appreciated informally. Reliability should be visible, scored, and paid.

## Slide 10 - Bonus Economics

This is the bridge from product logic to financial logic.

The current base model costs about 3.51 thousand pounds per NBA shift for 24 active gamers. The new calculator model costs about 4.51 thousand per shift, including the active model and expected standby cover.

Across a full NBA year, that adds about 732 thousand pounds versus the base-only model.

But one percentage point of reliability improvement protects around 942 cancellations a year, worth about 942 thousand pounds of recovered revenue. So the new model pays for itself at 0.78 percentage points of reliability improvement.

That is the strongest argument for the trial: the hurdle is not heroic. It is less than one point.

## Slide 11 - Projected Impact

This slide gives two views of the upside.

The conservative view uses title-specific recovery rates: NBA at 650 pounds, EAFC at 350, and eAMF at 400. The baseline view uses 1,000 pounds per cancellation.

Both tell the same story. At one point, the model already protects meaningful revenue. At five points, the upside is large enough to justify serious operational attention.

The numbers also leave out softer upside: partner confidence, better retention, lower gamer churn, and a cleaner product story.

## Slide 12 - The Ask

The ask is a six-week NBA operating strategy trial.

Scope it tightly. Use NBA first. Use Night Studios 5 and 6 as the controlled cell. Measure cancellation rate, SPS stability, bonus predictability, on-call usage, and gamer satisfaction every week.

If the gates pass, expand to full NBA Night first. If the mechanics need tuning, adjust and run the cell again. If the evidence does not support it, stop before wider disruption.

The decision is not whether to change everything tomorrow. The decision is whether to run a disciplined trial on the clearest revenue lever in the product.

## Closing

My closing line would be:

This is a reliability model disguised as a tournament model. If we make the game worth showing up for, we protect the feed. If we protect the feed, we protect revenue. The six-week NBA trial is the controlled way to prove that before scaling it further.
