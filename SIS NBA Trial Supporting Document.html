NBA TRIAL · APPENDIX

The operating detail behind the trial.

Appendix to the 11-slide NBA trial deck · Studios 5–6 · Division 3 · Night shift

This document consolidates the operational detail so the deck can stay concise: the qualitative case for change, the shift and stagger mechanics, the corrected fatigue-safe rotation, the roster and cadence model, the SPS progression system, the trial's measurement design, and the phase-2 economics that only become relevant if the trial's gates pass. Every figure below is a working assumption unless stated otherwise.

01 · PURPOSE & SCOPE

A six-week, no-cost trial — nothing more, nothing less.

This document backs a six-week trial of operating changes run on Studios 5–6, Division 3, with 8 gamers across 42 nights. It uses existing pay and existing staffing throughout — £0 incremental payroll. Nothing about base pay, headcount, or shift length changes during the trial itself; what changes is format, scheduling and visible progression, all layered onto the current operation at no added cost.

The trial is NBA-only. EAFC and eAMF are not touched by this proposal and would only inherit any part of this structure after NBA proves the model out on its own numbers.

02 · THE PROBLEM ON THE FLOOR

Cancellations come from a handful of recurring, ordinary causes.

None of this is exotic. Firsthand reporting from the floor groups the causes into a small set of recurring patterns — described here qualitatively, not as a per-cause cost breakdown.

Late / no-show

The ~10-minute grace window

If a player isn't present within roughly ten minutes of the scheduled time, the fixture is voided. The studio simply prepares the next scheduled fixture — the 32-minute cadence makes moving on easy — but the voided fixture itself is the loss.

Technical

Faults and random crashes

Tech issues and game crashes before, during or after a match start, not resolved in time to keep the fixture live.

Other

Ops and people-management issues

A residual bucket of other technical- and people-management-driven disruptions that also void fixtures.

No-shows happen very often — sometimes several people in a single day. When a player is absent, the schedule is amended: that player is dropped, and the remaining three rotate against each other unless a suitable replacement is found in time. These 3-man operations are widely complained about on the floor.

Base pay is generally considered alright, but the current bonus structure and the existing ELO-style ranking are ridiculed — neither carries much credibility with gamers today.

People also push to cancel strategically to gain break time. This is best read as a format failure rather than an individual one: the current format gives cancelling as the only real pressure valve, so some of it is the system working as designed, just not as intended.

Some come in sick rather than lose pay or standing, which harms everyone else on the floor that day — this is expected to ease as the operation stabilises. Nothing has ever been tried properly to address any of this.

The one number that matters here
£4.71M/yr

At a 5% cancellation rate (working assumption) against target NBA volume, and £1,000 of revenue lost per cancellation (working assumption), the pool of revenue at stake is approximately £4.71M per year. This is the only problem-money figure in this document — causes above are qualitative, not separately costed.

03 · SHIFT MECHANICS

The 24-hour clock, the stagger, and the divisions.

Two 12.5-hour shifts split the day. Day runs 06:30–19:00 across 4 studios (S5–S6 offline on Day). Night runs 18:30–07:00 across all 6 studios. Every match is a 32-minute slot (4×5-minute quarters, up to 4×3-minute overtime periods).

What this grid honestly claims — and nothing more:

  • ·Each gamer plays 11 of 22 matches (50%), with a hard cap of 3 consecutive matches.
  • ·All four triple-blocks (one per gamer) are front-loaded into matches 1–11 — the first half of the shift.
  • ·The entire second half of the shift (matches 12–22) runs on blocks of 2 or fewer — no triples after the midpoint.
  • ·Every gamer gets a rest of at least 2 matches immediately after their triple-block.
  • ·All six possible pairings (AB, AC, AD, BC, BD, CD) appear across the shift, each 3 or 4 times — near-balanced, giving managers room to steer marquee matchups toward the 4-count pairs.
  • ·One gamer per studio unavoidably carries two single-match blocks — the shift opener and the shift finale. This is the remainder of an odd 11-match load per gamer, placed at the shift edges where fatigue impact is lowest, not hidden inside the run.
  • ·Studios 4–6 use the 21-match variant: the same 22-match sequence with match 22 dropped, so two gamers play 10 matches that night instead of 11.

06 · SPS & PROGRESSION

A corrected scoring formula, and a career ladder above it.

SPS (the visible score behind rank) replaces the current ELO-style ranking, which gamers do not find credible. The formula below is the corrected version — weights are working values, to be regressed on trial data once it exists.

SPS formula
SPS = 0.45 × event_density + 0.25 × narrative + 0.15 × reliability + 0.15 × settlement_accuracy
0.45 weight

Event density

Fixtures completed versus scheduled, including delays.

0.25 weight

Narrative

Quarter-end lead changes, drop-offs, blowouts. A cancellation counts as a narrative penalty only for the gamer at fault — not for their opponent.

0.15 weight

Reliability

On-time starts, zero voids, feed uptime.

0.15 weight

Settlement accuracy

Clean market close, zero disputes.

SPS position is driven by an 8-week rolling average. There are no seasonal resets — full career history is retained, and promotions or relegations happen from rolling-threshold crossings rather than a periodic reshuffle.

07 · TRIAL DESIGN & MEASUREMENT

Five gates, measured every week, for six weeks.

What actually changes during the trial: (1) a tournament format on S5–S6, with promotion/relegation stakes per shift; (2) the 10-minute stagger and the corrected fatigue-safe rotation; (3) SPS scored and published as a visible rank covering skill, reliability and attendance.

GATE 01

Cancel / void rate falls materially

S5–S6 cancel/void rate falls materially below the ~5% baseline. Target: below 3%.

GATE 02

Fewer 3-man operations & late-start voids

Fewer 3-man fallback operations and fewer late-start voids than baseline.

GATE 03

SPS shows visible tier separation

By week 5, the SPS distribution across the 8 trial gamers shows visible tier separation.

GATE 04

100% retention, stable satisfaction

All 8 gamers complete the trial; satisfaction is stable or improving on a short weekly survey.

GATE 05

Schedule adherence

The stagger and rotation run as designed, week over week.

Week-by-week arc

Week 1

Baseline capture

Capture baseline cancel/void rate, 3-man-operation frequency and satisfaction before the new format changes anything.

Week 2

Format live

The tournament ladder, 10-minute stagger, corrected rotation and published SPS all run live on S5–S6.

Week 3

Stratification visible

Early SPS separation begins to show; gates 1–2 trend against baseline.

Week 4

Stress under normal pressure

The format runs under ordinary operational pressure — no-shows, tech faults, normal churn — not a curated best case.

Week 5

Sample closes

The measurement sample locks across all five gates, including the required week-5 SPS tier-separation check.

Week 6

Decision gate

Full retrospective against all five gates; leadership decides expand, tune & re-run, or stop.

Week-6 decision options

Option A

Expand

Gates pass — roll the format out to full NBA Night.

Option B

Tune & re-run

Partial pass — adjust mechanics and run another six-week cycle before expanding.

Option C

Stop

Gates fail — revert to the current operation; no further cost was ever incurred.

The 0.78pp break-even assumes £1,000 of revenue lost per NBA cancellation. At a more conservative £650 per cancellation, break-even rises to approximately 1.2pp. Which of these is closer to reality is precisely what the trial is designed to surface — it is not assumed in advance.

Reinvestment, once revenue recovery is proven

After the trial demonstrates revenue recovery, a further phase-2 step is to reinvest in gamer development — Analysts and Trainers, a role that has already shown good impact on lower-skilled gamers in the past.
